# Common Planner Audit & MyCurricula Kill/Pivot/Proceed Decision

## TL;DR — Recommendation: **PIVOT (Don't Kill, Don't Proceed As Planned)**

**The honest verdict:** Common Planner is roughly 75–80% of the K-12 single-teacher lesson planner you set out to build, and they've been polishing that 75–80% for **17 years** (founded 2009). Your initial gut reaction — "it seems to do almost everything my program would do and it looks great and uncluttered" — is substantially correct. Building MyCurricula head-on against Common Planner at $24/teacher/year, as a part-time solo founder in Qatar, is a path with a realistic ceiling somewhere around $10K–$40K ARR in 24–36 months. That is not a business; that is an expensive hobby with a tax-form attached.

**However**, Common Planner has three concrete, structural gaps that map almost perfectly onto your context:

1. **Their "Bump" is the exact destructive train-car cascade you wanted to fix.** Confirmed verbatim in their help docs: *"Days are like train cars on a track, they keep bumping or pulling the next day until they get to a blank day."* This is identical to Planbook.com. Your non-destructive Catch-up is a real, demoable wedge.
2. **Zero international-curriculum support.** All public marketing references CCSS, "50 state standards," and specific US state standards (Virginia SOLs, Georgia, etc.). No mention of IB, Cambridge, MOEHE/Qatar, NZ Curriculum, or Australian Curriculum anywhere in their site, help docs, or testimonials. Unsurprising — they're Baltimore-based, Abell Foundation–backed, US public-school built.
3. **No fork/personal-copy concept.** Their permission model (Can View / Can Comment / Can Edit / Can Edit & Manage) is Google-Docs–style co-editing on the *same* planbook. The only "personal copy" path is a manual "Copy" of a lesson or a once-per-year "copy the entire planbook" rollover. Your master→fork→merge architecture would be genuinely different — *but only matters in a department-team or school-wide context, not for solo teachers.*

**The right move is not to abandon MyCurricula and not to ship it as planned. Re-scope it as an international-schools / non-Western-calendar planner with first-class fork/merge for grade-level teams, and treat US teachers as out-of-scope.** This eliminates head-on competition with Common Planner (and Planbook, and Chalk.com) and puts you adjacent to Toddle/ManageBac in the international space — where there is still meaningful unserved demand below their school-license price points (especially for non-IB international schools, Cambridge schools, MOEHE Qatar national schools, and small private schools).

If that pivot isn't appealing, the next-best paths are listed in §5. The "proceed as planned, head-on against Common Planner" path is the only one I'd straight-up advise against.

### Collaboration model — the critical question, answered

From their help docs, **verbatim**:

> *"Can View – They can see your lessons and copy them into their own planbook.
> Can Comment – They can see, copy, and leave comments on your lessons.
> Can Edit – They can see, copy, leave comments, and change your lessons. Great for co-planners that want to write lessons together!
> Can Edit & Manage – They can see, copy, leave comments, change your lessons, and change all your planbook's settings."*

This is **unambiguously Google-Docs–style co-editing on the same single planbook**. There is no "personal copy that auto-forks." The closest analog is the **"Copy them into their own planbook"** action, which is a manual, explicit, snapshot-in-time copy — once copied, there is no relationship between the source and the copy. No merge. No re-sync. No notification when the master changes.

**Group planbook vs shared planbook:** A "shared planbook" is one planbook with multiple collaborators on it (Pro feature, up to 5; School tier, unlimited). A "group planbook" (or "school planbook") in the Schools tier is an organizational container — admins can browse any teacher's planbook in one place, and teachers can be organized into teams/sub-teams. It's still the same underlying object model: **one canonical planbook per teacher, with permissions granted to others on it.** There is no master→derivative relationship anywhere.

**This means your master/fork/merge architecture is a genuine differentiator — but only for the specific use case of a grade-level team where a head-of-department wants to publish a canonical scope-and-sequence and have individual teachers personalize it without breaking the link.** For a solo teacher, the difference is invisible. For a co-teaching pair, "Can Edit" is honestly fine. For a school-wide curriculum, it matters — and that's exactly the market that already pays Toddle/Atlas/ManageBac four-figure sums per school.

### Bump / lesson rescheduling — **destructive cascade confirmed**

Their help article *"Move Lessons"* states explicitly:

> *"Click 'Bump All Lessons' Note: By bumping all your lessons for that day, it not only bumps that day but also bumps any other planned days before that."*
>
> *"Days are like train cars on a track, they keep bumping or pulling the next day until they get to a blank day."*

This is the **identical destructive cascade behavior of Planbook.com**. Your "non-destructive Catch-up with destination picker" is real, defensible, and demonstrable as a side-by-side comparison. Many testimonials on commonplanner.com/testimonials specifically praise "bump" as a feature — but the same testimonials reveal users treat it as "good enough" because they're not aware better is possible. **This is your strongest single feature wedge.** It's also small — most teachers bump 5–10 times a year max, so it's not by itself a $24/year purchase decision.

### AI features

- Basic gets "AI Powered Lesson Assistant"

Inferences from competitor positioning and their marketing language ("brainstorm," "draft"): this is almost certainly a constrained text-generation feature scoped to lesson plan section drafting, **not** a multi-tool suite. They are nowhere near MagicSchool (80+ tools), Brisk (30+), or Eduaide (100+ resource types) in AI breadth. If a teacher wants serious AI, they bolt on MagicSchool/Brisk/Eduaide for free. **AI is not Common Planner's moat; the planbook is.**

Your planned Phase-3 AI (curriculum import / standards autotagging / intervention suggestions) is *more interesting* than what Common Planner ships today — but every one of the AI-only competitors will likely have shipped similar features before MyCurricula reaches Phase 3.

## 3. The Strategic Question, Answered Directly

### 3a. Is there realistic differentiation room left?

**For US K-12 solo teachers: essentially no.** Common Planner Basic is free, polished, and ex-teacher-built. Anyone who tries MyCurricula in the US after trying Common Planner will ask "why is this $24 when that's free?" and your answer ("better bump, fork/merge for teams") won't move them.

**For international/non-Western-calendar schools and teams: yes, meaningful room.** Common Planner has literally zero presence there, Toddle is priced at the school level (not affordable for individual teachers or small schools), Atlas/Rubicon is curriculum-mapping-not-planning, and ManageBac is IB-specific. The wedge is real but the segment is small.

### 3b. Is fork-and-merge actually meaningfully different in practice?

**Honest answer:** Only when there is a "master" — a head-of-department, curriculum coordinator, or grade-level lead who maintains a canonical scope-and-sequence that other teachers personalize. For **solo teachers**, the experience of "Edit permission on shared planbook" vs "fork from master" is indistinguishable in 95% of workflows. For a **co-teaching pair**, again, mostly indistinguishable.

It matters in exactly two scenarios:
1. **Whole-grade-level publishing** at a school: HoD writes the master, 6 Grade 5 teachers each have a personal copy that auto-syncs from master but preserves their tweaks. Common Planner cannot do this elegantly; you'd be the first.
2. **Mid-year master updates**: When the curriculum coordinator changes Unit 4 on March 15, every teacher's planbook should reflect "here's what changed; here's what you'd lose if you re-sync; here's a diff." Common Planner has no diff/merge concept.

This is a real **school-tier** feature, not an individual-teacher feature — which means it monetizes at the school plan, not the individual $24/yr plan. And selling school plans as a solo Qatar-based founder is a 12–18-month enterprise sale per school. **So fork/merge is "real differentiation that is hard to monetize as a solo founder."**

### 3c. Is the Qatar/Sun–Thu/Ramadan/multi-framework angle big enough to justify a whole product?

**Big enough to justify a niche product (yes). Big enough to justify the product you originally scoped (no).** A rough TAM:

- ~12,000 international schools globally (ISC Research figure widely cited).
- ~50% use English-medium Western-style curricula (IB, Cambridge, AP, US-style); the others use national curricula adapted.
- Of those, maybe 30–40% are on non-Western school weeks (Gulf, parts of Africa, Israel) and/or observe Ramadan.
- Add national K-12 schools in MENA using MOEHE/equivalent: tens of thousands more, but lower ARPU.
- Total addressable teachers in the "non-Western calendar AND wants English-language planner" segment: ~200K–400K teachers globally, very rough.

At $24/yr individual: even 1% capture = $48K–96K ARR. At 5% capture (excellent for a niche tool over 3–5 years) = $240K–480K. At $10/teacher/month school plan, 50 schools at avg 40 teachers = $240K ARR.

**This is the only re-scoping where MyCurricula's unit economics actually make sense as a real business and not a hobby.**

### 3d. Could Common Planner ship fork-and-merge in 6–12 months if you gained traction?

**Yes, easily — if they wanted to.** It's a data-model change, not a research problem. Git-like derivation is well-understood software.

**But will they?** Probably not within 12 months, for three reasons:
1. They're a small team that ships steadily, not aggressively. 17 years to get to where they are.
2. Their current customer base (US public schools) doesn't ask for this — they ask for "share my plans with admin." Common Planner has built exactly what their customer asked for.
3. Adding fork/merge to a 17-year-old codebase that assumed one-planbook-per-teacher is genuinely hard.

If you reach $50K+ MRR with fork/merge as a known differentiator, **they will absolutely consider it in their next major refactor or as a school-tier feature.** Plan for a 12–24 month window of structural advantage, not a permanent moat.

### 3e. What would you actually learn by building this anyway?

**Real skills you'd build (independent of commercial outcome):**
- Production Next.js + Supabase at non-trivial scale (RLS, real-time, full-text search, multi-tenant)
- CRDT or event-sourcing thinking if you actually implement fork/merge well (this is genuinely deep)
- B2B2C sales motion (teacher PLG → school plan upsell)
- International payments, tax, Stripe Tax / Paddle, multi-currency
- Edtech-specific concerns: FERPA/COPPA/GDPR data handling, parent-facing surfaces, age-of-consent
- The brutal lesson that most edtech buyers buy on relationship and trust, not features

**Real skills you might fool yourself into thinking you're learning but actually aren't:**
- "Market research" — you've already done it; more isn't the bottleneck
- Generic "engineering" — building any app teaches generic engineering; this one isn't special
- "Founder experience" — only counts if you ship to paying customers, which the head-on path makes unlikely

**Net:** Learning value is real (~$30K–$60K equivalent of skill development if you actually finish a v1 with paying users). Business value, on the head-on path, is much lower. **Separate these honestly: if you'd value 12 months of part-time work at $30K–$60K of pure skill upside even if revenue is ~$5K, that's a legitimate decision. Just don't tell yourself it's a business when it's a bootcamp.**

## 4. Competitive Landscape Update

### Updates that should change the prior business plan

**1. Common Planner is more polished than the prior plan likely assumed.** A "hybrid PLG-to-school-plan path at $24/year" doesn't credibly survive contact with Common Planner Basic being free and Pro at $71.88. You're not undercutting Common Planner; you're undercutting their *Pro tier*, while their *free tier* eats your funnel. The prior plan's pricing strategy needs to be re-examined: either go cheaper (which is hard at $24) or go more expensive on a niche where Common Planner doesn't compete (international schools at $40–80/teacher/yr with a school-plan upsell at $400–$1,500/year).

**2. AI lesson-plan generation has been fully commoditized in 2025.** MagicSchool (80+ tools, generous free tier, $99/yr Plus, district plans, SOC 2 / FERPA / COPPA), Brisk Teaching (Chrome extension into Google Workspace, $9.99/mo Pro), Eduaide.ai (100+ resource types), Diffit (differentiation focus), Curipod (engagement), Khanmigo (free for US teachers), Education Copilot. **Your Phase 3 AI is no longer a moat in 2026; it's table stakes.** Common Planner has only a thin wrapper over OpenAI/Anthropic, but so will you. The actual moat in AI today is district procurement posture (FERPA/COPPA data residency, no-training-on-student-data, SOC 2), not model quality.

### Tier 1 — Strongest fit

**1. International Schools Lesson Planner (MyCurricula re-scoped)**

- **Why it works:** Tiny feature surface, viral within schools, no head-on competition, easy to integrate with anyone
- **Risk:** Hard to grow past $30K–$50K ARR without expanding scope

### Tier 2 — Strong but harder

**3. Multi-Framework Standards-Tagging API Service**

**4. AI Curriculum-Audit / Coverage-Gap Tool**
- **Concept:** Read a school's existing planbook (Common Planner, Toddle, even Google Drive folders of Word docs), tag against the standards framework, and produce a coverage report: which standards taught, which missed, which over-taught, drift over time. Sell as a school-administrator tool ("AI accreditation prep").
- **Wedge:** Common Planner shows what's been planned but doesn't *audit*. Atlas does mapping but doesn't ingest others' planners.
- **Pricing:** $500–$2,500/school/year
- **Why it works:** Connects to accreditation cycles, which schools *budget* for. Could integrate as a Common Planner extension.

## 6. 30 / 60 / 90 Day Plan (Assuming Tier 1, Path #1: International Schools Pivot)
