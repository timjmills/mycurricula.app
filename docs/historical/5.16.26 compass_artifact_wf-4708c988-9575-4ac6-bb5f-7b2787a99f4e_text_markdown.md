# Integrated Business Plan & Viability Analysis: The Curriculum Planner App
*A reconciled, evidence-based revision incorporating both prior strategic frames*

---

## TL;DR — Recommended Path

**Build the product as a teacher-paid Product-Led Growth (PLG) consumer SaaS first, with a deliberately engineered upsell ramp to school site licenses beginning in Year 3.** This is closest to Frame A, but it explicitly adopts Frame B's compliance and corporate infrastructure as gated milestones rather than Day-1 burdens.

- **Year 0–1 (Phase 1A/1B):** Free closed beta with the 4-teacher Grade 5 team in Qatar. No payments. No LLC until you accept the first paying customer.
- **Year 1–2 (Phase 2):** Open PLG launch at **$3/month or $24/year per teacher** (NOT $18 — the math doesn't work at $18; see §III). Free Viewer tier capped at ~5 seats per paid teacher rather than unlimited. AI features gated behind paid.
- **Year 2–3:** When 5+ teachers from the same school are paying, trigger a "School Plan" upsell ($1,200–$3,500/school/year flat) that absorbs individual subs. This unlocks the compliance investment (DPA, SOC 2 Type I, VPAT) as a financed expense, not a speculative one.
- **Year 3–5:** Decide based on real data whether to lean further into the school motion (Frame B) or stay teacher-paid forever (Frame A pure).

**Realistic ceiling for a solo, part-time, Qatar-based founder with Claude Code as engineering assistance:** $80K–$400K ARR within 4–5 years, with a ~25–35% probability of clearing $50K ARR (the threshold for seriously considering leaving teaching) and a ~5–10% probability of clearing $500K ARR. Reaching $1M ARR solo and part-time is realistically <3%. This is a viable lifestyle SaaS, not a venture-scale company at the founder's current capacity.

**The most important warning:** Frame A's $18/teacher price and "unlimited free viewers" are too aggressive once AI inference, storage, payment processing, and even minimal customer-support time are subtracted. The numbers work at ~$24–$36/year, not $18. Frame B's school-license-only path is operationally correct but cycle-time-wrong: a solo, full-time-teaching, Qatar-based founder cannot close $5K school deals while teaching. The hybrid path is the only one that respects both constraints.

## Key Findings (10 most important)

1. **Frame A's factual claims about Planbook are largely accurate.** Planbook.com publicly markets itself as $20/year, "over one million teachers, one billion lessons," 1,200+ user-requested features, and a 90-day free trial — verified on planbook.com, the company's LinkedIn, app stores, and help docs. PlanbookEDU raised from $25 to **$30/year on May 1, 2025** (their first price increase since 2011). These are real, mature competitors.

2. **The destructive "Bump Forward" cascade is real and is a genuine UX failure.** Planbook's own help documentation confirms "Bump Forward… will also shift forward **all** future lessons for the class accordingly," and Bump Backward will *DELETE* lessons in the destination date range. A live user-voice ticket titled "fix the cascade effect from bumping a lesson" remains open on planbook.uservoice.com. Frame A's framing of this as an exploitable gap is correct.

3. **Frame A's "three rigid collaboration modes" framing is outdated.** Planbook v12 (mid-2025) shipped a refreshed UI, SSO, in-app messaging, and Change History (undo). More importantly, **Common Planner (formerly Common Curriculum) is the more direct competitive threat than Frame A acknowledged**, not Planbook. Common Planner already supports invite-based collaborators with **Edit / Comment / View permissions**, group planbooks for grade-level teams, and a "Cc for Schools" tier with shared templates. They have also shipped AI features and a "bump" feature. The novelty gap on basic collaboration is narrower than Frame A assumed.

4. **The fork-and-merge architecture remains a genuine differentiator — but only because no one else has shipped it, not because no one could.** None of the current K-12 planners (Planbook, PlanbookEDU, Common Planner, Atlas, ManageBac, Toddle, Curriculum Trak) implements Git-style fork-and-merge with non-destructive personal edits over a shared master. Common Planner's "shared planbook" gives co-edit, comment, or view — but a teacher who edits a shared planbook is editing the *shared* artifact, not creating a personal divergent copy. **Caveat:** that novelty is software-engineering novelty, not patent novelty. Post-*Alice* (Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank, 2014), software-method patents on "fork and merge applied to lesson planning" would almost certainly be invalidated as abstract ideas with generic computer implementation. The moat is execution + brand + workflow lock-in, not IP.

5. **The AI lesson-tools market is already crowded and consolidated around MagicSchool, Brisk, and Eduaide — but these are *generators*, not *planners*.** MagicSchool: 5M+ educators, 13,000+ school/district partnerships, 160 countries, $8.33/month Plus tier, SOC 2 + FERPA/COPPA compliant. Brisk Teaching: $99.99/yr Pro, free tier with 20+ tools, Chrome-extension-first. Eduaide: $5.99/month. None does curriculum management or year-long pacing. They sit *adjacent* to the planner space and are likely future partners or acquirers, not direct competitors. Frame A's "AI lesson generation" feature is table-stakes-becoming-commoditized; it should not be the lead differentiator.

6. **K-12 PLG works but slowly, and the named exemplars (Newsela, Nearpod) ended up monetizing only through districts, not teachers.** Newsela's own FAQ explicitly states: "Only schools and districts can purchase Newsela's premium products." Nearpod migrated from individual sales to school/district pricing — its terms even offer prorated refunds when a teacher's individual license is absorbed into a school license. MagicSchool kept individual paid tiers but Enterprise now dominates revenue. **This is precisely the hybrid path. The pattern is real, and it is the dominant K-12 PLG outcome.** Planbook.com is the rare counter-example that stayed teacher-paid for 15 years; even it now offers school accounts.

7. **The ESSER cliff has already happened and has compressed K-12 vendor spending — but in 2026 the market is "sustainability-driven, not stimulus-driven."** ARP ESSER ($122B of the total $189.5B) had to be obligated by Sept 30, 2024 and liquidated by Jan 28, 2025 (with late-liquidation extensions to March 28/30, 2026). The Edunomics Lab and K-12 Dive both confirm vendor contracts are being cut first, before personnel. **This is unambiguously bad news for Frame B**: districts have less discretionary edtech budget now than at any point in six years and are scrutinizing every new line item. It is moderately bad for Frame A too: districts are cracking down on "shadow IT" individual-teacher subs, which can suppress organic teacher purchasing.

8. **At Frame A's $18/year price, unit economics are positive but thin, and turn *negative* if AI usage is uncapped.** Detailed math in §III. At $18/yr a teacher with average AI usage yields ~$3–$8 of net contribution margin per teacher per year after Stripe, AI inference, storage, and minimal support time. That works only at high volume (5,000+ paid teachers) and breaks immediately if you give power users uncapped Claude Sonnet access. **$24–$36/year is the defensible price; $18 should be reserved as a launch promo only.**

9. **Frame A's "unlimited free Viewer accounts" is strategically interesting but operationally dangerous.** Admin/support-staff read-only visibility *can* create pull toward a school site license (Slack and Notion both prove this in horizontal SaaS). But "unlimited" creates abuse vectors (one paying teacher = entire school free read access), and importantly *cannibalizes* the most natural next-paying-user (the co-teacher who edits, the special-ed teacher who annotates). The right model is **3–5 free Viewer seats per paid teacher**, with Comment/Annotate as a paid privilege (the Notion Guest model adapted).

10. **The product name "Grade 5 Curriculum Planner" is unregistrable as a trademark and must be renamed before any public launch.** "Grade 5" is generic; "Curriculum Planner" is descriptive. The USPTO and any competent trademark attorney will reject it under §2(e)(1) of the Lanham Act. Frame B was correct on this; Frame A did not address it.

## I. Reconciling Frame A and Frame B

Frame A and Frame B are not the same business. They differ in price by ~100× per teacher and in customer-acquisition motion by an order of magnitude in time and effort. They cannot both be true for the same product at the same time.

| Dimension | Frame A (PLG / $18) | Frame B (School License / $1,500–$8,000) | Hybrid (Recommended) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Customer | Individual teacher | School / district | Individual → School over time |
| Buyer | Same person who uses it | Principal / Director of Curriculum / CTO | Teacher first; admin second |
| ACV | $18–$30 | $1,500–$8,000 | $24/seat → $1,500–$5,000 site |
| Sales cycle | Self-serve, minutes | 6–18 months | Self-serve → 3–6 months |
| Founder time per customer | <5 min | 20–60 hours | <5 min → 5–10 hours |
| Compliance burden Day 1 | Low (terms, basic privacy) | High (DPA, FERPA, SOC 2, VPAT) | Low Day 1, scaled Year 2–3 |
| Cash to first paid customer | <$500 | $30K–$75K | <$1,000 |
| Time to first paid customer | 1–6 months | 12–36 months | 1–6 months |
| Time to $10K MRR | 24–60 months | 36–60 months | 24–48 months |
| Realistic max ARR (solo, part-time) | $100K–$600K | $200K–$1M | $80K–$400K (median) |
| Failure mode | Slow drip of teacher subs; can't out-market MagicSchool's free | Sales cycle exceeds founder's available time | Operationally complex; switching billing mid-stream |
| Probability solo/part-time reaches it | ~40% to $25K ARR | ~10% to $100K ARR | ~30% to $50K ARR |

**Why Frame A alone is insufficient:**
- At $18/yr you need ~5,500 paid teachers to hit $100K ARR. At 3% free-to-paid conversion (already optimistic for K-12), that's ~185,000 free signups. Planbook took 10+ years to reach 1M users. As a solo, part-time founder competing against Planbook's brand, Common Planner's incumbency, and MagicSchool's marketing budget, **getting to 185K signups within five years is realistically a <15% scenario.**
- Even when PLG works in K-12, the monetization eventually shifts to district contracts (Newsela, Nearpod, MagicSchool all followed this trajectory).

**Why Frame B alone is infeasible *for this founder right now*:**
- A solo, full-time-teaching, Qatar-based founder cannot run a $5K school-deal sales cycle. A typical international-schools deal involves a curriculum coordinator demo, an IT-security review, a DPA negotiation, a principal sign-off, and a finance approval over 3–9 months. You cannot fit that around grading homework in Doha at 9 pm.
- Frame B's $25K–$50K SOC 2 Type II investment is real and *not optional* for serious district sales (Toddle, MagicSchool, Common Planner all advertise SOC 2 / FERPA / GDPR / COPPA prominently). Spending that money before product-market fit is validated by paying teachers is a high-risk bet.

**Why the hybrid is the right answer for this specific founder:**
- It uses the *PLG motion* to absorb customer-acquisition cost (the next paid teacher Googles you or hears from a colleague), while building the *evidence* (school-level usage data, NPS, references) that makes the eventual school sale a 90-day cycle rather than 18 months.
- It pushes compliance investment from "speculative cost" to "financed expansion." You don't pay $25K for SOC 2 until you have, say, 200 paying teachers and a $7K school deal on the table that requires it.
- It matches Newsela/Nearpod/MagicSchool's actual evolutionary path. You are not pioneering this motion; you are repeating a known pattern.
- It preserves optionality: if PLG underperforms, you have a working product with real users to show prospective school buyers. If PLG over-performs, you can ride it indefinitely without ever doing enterprise sales.

## III. Unit Economics at $18/Teacher/Year — Why $24–$36 Is Defensible

### Per-paid-teacher annual math, fully shown

**Revenue per paid teacher per year:** $18.00

**Cost of payment processing (Stripe, US):** 2.9% + $0.30 → annual: $18 × 0.029 + $0.30 = **$0.82**. *Net after Stripe: $17.18.*

**AI inference (2026 Claude pricing: Haiku 4.5 $1/$5 per M tokens; Sonnet 4.6 $3/$15 per M tokens):**

Per the user's spec, AI features = curriculum import, standards auto-tag, intervention suggestions, lesson generation.

- **Standards auto-tag** (Haiku, runs per lesson, ~1.5K in + 300 out): 200 lessons × ($0.0015 + $0.0015) = **$0.60/yr**
- **Curriculum import** (Sonnet, 2–3×/yr, ~30K in + 8K out): 3 × ($0.09 + $0.12) = **$0.63/yr**
- **Intervention suggestions** (Sonnet, ~5K in + 2K out, ~20 calls): 20 × ($0.015 + $0.030) = **$0.90/yr**
- **Lesson generation** (Sonnet, ~3K in + 2K out, ~50 generations): 50 × ($0.009 + $0.030) = **$1.95/yr**

**Realistic AI subtotal with prompt caching (90% savings on repeated context): ~$3.00–$5.00/yr per active teacher.**

⚠️ **Without budget controls, a power user generating 500 lessons via Sonnet 4.6 uncached could hit $20–$40 in AI cost alone — exceeding the entire $18 subscription.** Per-user AI quotas are non-negotiable. Use the cheap Haiku for autotagging and standards work; reserve Sonnet for the genuinely complex generation steps; aggressively use prompt caching for system prompts and standards-framework context.

**Storage (Supabase Pro $25/mo base = 100GB storage + 250GB egress; overage ~$0.021/GB storage, $0.09/GB egress):**

The spec's storage envelope is 25 MB × 10 files × 25–35 lessons × 40 weeks ≈ **7–10 GB per *Master plan***. But the Master/Personal fork architecture means heavy files (PDFs, images, videos) live *only on the Master*; each personal fork is a lightweight pointer + text diff. For a typical 4-teacher team: one shared 8 GB Master + four personal forks of 100–250 MB each = ~9 GB per team of 4 = **~2.25 GB per paid teacher (team-amortized)**.

- 1,000 paid teachers = ~2.25 TB total storage = ~$50/mo overage on top of base = **~$0.50–$1.00/yr per teacher**
- Egress: light, mostly served via Cloudflare edge cache. ~$0.20/yr per teacher amortized

**Hosting (Cloudflare Pages free + Supabase Pro $25/mo + domain + monitoring + transactional email):**
- Supabase Pro: $300/yr base + scaling
- Cloudflare Pages: free tier sufficient through ~100K monthly users
- Domain, email (Postmark/Resend), monitoring (Sentry free tier): ~$300–$500/yr
- Total fixed Year 1: ~$600–$1,200/yr → **~$1.00/yr/teacher** at 1,000 teachers

**Customer support time (the hidden killer):**
- Even with great docs, expect ~1 ticket/teacher/year × ~10 min
- Valued at $0 (founder absorbs): $0
- Valued at $30/hr (teacher-salary equivalent): **~$5.00/yr/teacher**
- At 1,000 teachers = ~167 hrs/yr = ~3 hrs/week — manageable for a part-time founder

At Frame A's claimed 3–8% free-to-paid conversion, that's 100,000–1,000,000 free users. **For a solo part-time founder, this is the *bound* of viability, not the center of the distribution.**

## IV. The PLG Motion in K-12 — Realistic, Not Optimistic

### What the evidence actually shows

**Companies that started teacher-paid and proved K-12 PLG works (and what happened after):**

- **Nearpod**: Started teacher-paid. Migrated to school/district sales; offers prorated refunds on individual licenses when a school adopts. Acquired by Renaissance Learning in 2021.
- **Newsela**: Started teacher-paid. As of 2026, the FAQ explicitly states "**Only schools and districts can purchase Newsela's premium products**." Effectively abandoned the individual-teacher purchase path. 3.3M registered teachers, 40M registered students.
- **MagicSchool**: 5M+ educators, 13,000+ school/district contracts, 160 countries. Free + $8.33/mo Plus + Enterprise. Enterprise now drives revenue. Started PLG and migrated within 24–36 months.
- **Brisk Teaching**: Still mostly teacher-paid ($99.99/yr Pro). Custom school/district plans launched 2024–2025. Currently mid-transition.
- **Common Planner**: Free tier + Pro + "Cc for Schools" school-level tier. Active hybrid.
- **Planbook.com**: Stayed mostly teacher-paid at $20 for 15+ years. School accounts optional, not the main motion. **The rare counter-example — but they are now adding admin/district features, suggesting drift toward hybrid.**

**The pattern: K-12 PLG companies almost universally start teacher-paid and add (or migrate to) a school-paid tier within 3–7 years.** The teacher-paid stage is real and indispensable (it's how you generate references, NPS, and PMF). It rarely scales past low-seven-figure ARR by itself.

### Realistic conversion rates and viral coefficient

- **Free → paid conversion in B2C SaaS:** 1–5% typical.
- **K-12 specifically:** lower than horizontal SaaS due to teacher personal-money sensitivity. Realistic: **2–4%**.
- For specialty tools where free is genuinely useful (Common Planner, Planbook 90-day trial): 5–10% but with smaller funnels.
- **Use 3% as your base-case planning assumption.**

**Viral coefficient (k):** K-12 teacher viral coefficients are typically 0.3–0.8, almost never above 1.0 sustained. Teachers refer colleagues but rarely actively evangelize. The "grade-level team of 4" mechanic in this product is unusually well-suited because the product *requires* multi-teacher use to deliver its core value. Your effective k could be **1.0–1.5 within the first 90 days of any adoption** (one paying teacher pulls in 2–3 teammates via the trial-invite mechanic), then plateau as the team is fully covered.

### Funnel math at $24/yr

- **$50K ARR** ≈ 2,100 paid teachers. At 3% conversion: **~70,000 free signups**. Timeline: 24–36 months part-time.
- **$200K ARR** ≈ 8,300 paid teachers ≈ ~280,000 free signups. Timeline: 48–60 months minimum, with substantial paid acquisition or one viral hit.
- **$500K ARR** ≈ 21,000 paid teachers (PLG-only) OR 200 schools at $2,500 (hybrid). The hybrid path is dramatically faster at this scale.

## V. Legal / Compliance: What Frame A Missed and How to Stage It

### The good news Frame B overstated

If you store **no student PII** (names, grades, IEPs, attendance, contact info), most K-12 privacy laws **do not apply to your product**.

- **FERPA**: Applies to *student* educational records. If your product is a teacher's planning tool — curriculum content, pacing, standards tags, teacher notes — and student data never enters the system, FERPA does not regulate you. (This is the same posture Planbook.com operates under for individual teacher accounts.)
- **COPPA**: Applies to commercial websites collecting personal data from children under 13. If no students sign in, COPPA does not apply.
- **State student-data laws (NY Ed Law §2-d, California SOPIPA, etc.)**: Generally activate only when a *school or district* signs and student data is involved. Triggered by a DPA, not by individual teacher use.

**Practical implication:** As long as you keep students out of the database in Phase 1A–2, your day-one compliance burden is the same as any small SaaS: ToS, Privacy Policy, GDPR consent for EU teachers, basic data security. **Frame A was correct: PLG-only doesn't trigger the heavy compliance load.**

### What you DO need from Day 1 (~$2,000 total)

- Privacy policy + Terms of Service (Termly, Iubenda, or attorney for ~$500–$1,500). Must address EU GDPR for international-school teachers.
- Stripe Tax enabled for VAT/sales tax automatic handling.
- Basic data security: TLS everywhere, Supabase RLS configured correctly, no PII in logs, daily backups, password rules and Google SSO.
- Audit logging from Day 1 (already in your spec — keep it).
- Cookie banner if any analytics.

### What you need at the *site-license* stage (Year 2–3, ~$10K–$15K)

- DPA template (~$1,500 legal review).
- Sub-processor list publicly listed (Supabase, Cloudflare, Stripe, Anthropic/OpenAI).
- Student Data Privacy Consortium (SDPC) "National DPA" template adoption — free signal of trust.
- **SOC 2 Type I** (~$8K–$15K, ~3 months) — sufficient for most international schools and small districts.
- VPAT / WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility statement.
- GDPR Article 28 controller-processor terms in DPA.

### What you only need at scale (Year 4+, $25K–$50K)

- **SOC 2 Type II** ($25K–$50K, 12-month audit window). Trigger only when first $10K+ contract requires it.
- iKeepSafe COPPA Safe Harbor seal — only if you ever add student-facing features.
- State-by-state DPA negotiations for US public-school sales (avoidable if you stay international + private + charter).

**Net advantage of the hybrid:** Day-one compliance is ~$2K, not Frame B's implied $50K upfront. Compliance scales with revenue.

## VI. The Master/Personal Fork Moat — Real, but Narrower Than Frame A Claimed

Cross-referenced against direct competitor documentation:

| Competitor | Collaboration model | Has fork-and-merge? |
|---|---|---|
| **Planbook.com** | Link Lessons, Copy/Paste, Send To, Change History (v12). Shared planbooks via school accounts. | **No** — propagating edits, no personal divergent copy. |
| **PlanbookEDU** | File attachment, sharing, collaboration | **No** |
| **Common Planner (Common Curriculum)** | Add Collaborators with **Edit / Comment / View** permissions; shared planbook semantics; Cc for Schools group planbooks; AI integrated | **No** — edit access edits the *shared* planbook directly; no personal/master divergence |
| **Atlas / Atlas Next (Faria)** | Curriculum mapping with school-wide collaboration | **No** — built for curriculum maps, not pacing-level fork/merge |
| **ManageBac** | IB-program-focused unit planning | **No** |
| **Toddle** | "Collaborate on course overviews, unit plans, lesson plans, and assessments" — but shared documents | **No** |
| **Curriculum Trak** | Faith-based mapping; migrating to Atlas through Spring 2028 | **No** |
| **MagicSchool / Brisk / Eduaide** | AI generation; not planners | N/A |

**Conclusion: fork-and-merge is genuinely novel in K-12 curriculum planning as of 2026.** No competitor has shipped it. The closest analog is Common Planner's Edit/Comment/View permission model on shared planbooks, which is a permission model, not a version-control model.

### What kind of moat is it?

- **Patent moat:** Effectively zero. Post-*Alice*, software-method patents on "fork and merge applied to lesson planning" would be invalidated as abstract ideas + generic computer. **Do not spend money trying to patent.** Instead, **publish a defensive disclosure** on your company blog describing the architecture so a later entrant cannot patent it against you.
- **Engineering moat:** Modest. A capable Common Planner or Toddle team could ship fork-and-merge in 9–18 months if they prioritized it. They haven't — not because it's technically hard, but because of product-design choice and the cost of breaking existing UX.
- **Brand / first-mover moat:** This is the real moat. If you ship fork-and-merge first and "the planner with the fork model" becomes term-of-art, you own the framing.
- **Workflow lock-in moat:** Strong if executed well. Once a 4-teacher team has 18 months of curriculum in your fork structure, migrating off is genuinely painful. This is your retention moat.

**Frame A's strategic claim that this is a moat is correct. Frame A's implicit claim that this is *defensible IP* is incorrect — it's defensible *execution* with *brand* and *lock-in*.**

## VII. The Catch-Up / Non-Destructive Bump — Real and Underrated

### Verified

- Planbook's Bump Forward cascades all downstream lessons.
- Bump Backward DELETES lessons in the destination date range.
- Planbook offers "Lock Lesson to Date" as workaround, but their docs say "not something we recommend for every day use."
- UserVoice ticket **"fix the cascade effect from bumping a lesson"** remains open.
- Common Planner *also* has a "bump" feature ("Jada bumps her lessons around holidays and the surprise assemblies her school is so fond of throwing"). Public documentation does not specify whether it's destructive or has a carry-over destination picker. **Worth a live trial to verify** before claiming uniqueness in marketing.

### Is this a big deal to teachers?

Yes. Planbook user reviews repeatedly cite Bump pain: "If I extend a plan and my academic year is full, it deletes the last plan of the year. I wish there was a way to bump it without deleting it completely." This is a real, *acknowledged* pain. It's a defensible secondary differentiator after fork-and-merge.

**Recommendation:** Lead the product narrative with **fork-and-merge** (primary) + **non-destructive catch-up with destination picker** (supporting). Together they comprise the structural argument for "this is a different category of planner."

## IX. The Read-Only Viewer Tier — Reframed

Frame A: "Unlimited free Viewer accounts for special ed co-teachers, paraprofessionals, administrators, interventionists."

**Reality check:** A K-5 elementary school of ~30 teachers might have 8–12 paraprofessionals, 3–6 specialists, 2–4 administrators = 15–22 non-classroom adults who could be Viewers. If 4 grade-level teachers pay, "unlimited" means ~20 free accounts per 4 paid = 5:1 viewer-to-payer ratio. The entire school is using the product; only 4 are paying.

**Two failure modes:**
1. **Admin sees "everyone has it for free," doesn't see value in a site license.** Opposite of the desired PLG-to-enterprise pull.
2. **The most natural next paying user (co-teacher who annotates, special-ed teacher who modifies) is now a free Viewer with no upgrade path.**

**Better model — adapt the Notion Guest pattern:**
- **Paid teacher gets 5 free Viewer seats** (enough for grade-level admins, principal, instructional coach, one specialist)
- **Beyond 5: $6/seat/year Viewer add-on**, OR **$0/seat for "School Plan" customers**
- This makes Viewer count itself an upsell trigger. When a school hits ~25–40 viewer requests, it's natural to move them to a flat School Plan.
- Comments are *paid* (Editor / Commenter role). Viewing + reactions are free.

This preserves Frame A's PLG-pull mechanism without cannibalization.

## XII. The Hybrid Path in Detail

### Why this is the only path that fits the constraints

You can't be a solo enterprise seller in Qatar while teaching full-time. You can't reach $1M ARR purely at $24/teacher with 10 hrs/week. But you *can*:

1. Build the product on the PLG flywheel: self-serve onboarding, individual teachers paying $24/year, viral team invites doing customer acquisition for free.
2. When **5+ paying teachers from the same school** are detected, fire an automated email to the school's curriculum coordinator: *"You have 7 teachers at Lincoln Elementary using [product]. Would you like a School Plan that consolidates billing and adds 25 free Viewer seats?"*
3. Move them to a flat **$1,200–$3,500/school/year** based on size. The school upgrade absorbs individual subs.
4. Compliance investments (DPA, eventually SOC 2 Type I) are *financed* by these school deals, not speculative.

### Has anyone done this in K-12?

Yes — and it is the dominant K-12 PLG outcome:
- **MagicSchool**: teacher-PLG → Enterprise revenue dominance within 24 months
- **Nearpod**: same trajectory; acquired 2021
- **Newsela**: same; now school-only
- **Common Planner**: same, currently mid-transition
- **Planbook**: rare exception — stayed teacher-paid for 15 years, school accounts optional

**The hybrid is the *normal* path. PLG-forever is the exception.**

### Operational complexities to plan for

- **Billing transitions**: when a School Plan is signed, individual teacher subs must be prorated and refunded. Stripe handles this; Nearpod's policy is the template.
- **Identity model**: school plans likely use Google Workspace SSO + domain-based onboarding (any *@lincolnelementary.edu* signs up free).
- **Customer success time**: school customers expect a 15-min onboarding call. At 50 schools = 12 hrs/yr — manageable.
- **Procurement gatekeepers**: at school level (not district) and at international schools, gatekeepers are softer. A principal can typically approve a $2,500 line item without a 6-month process. **District-level deals should be avoided until Year 4+** — they're the procurement hell Frame B warned about.

### When the hybrid becomes "the worst of both worlds"

- Building the School Plan motion before PLG has produced 200+ paying teachers wastes time on enterprise sales with no leverage.
- Building the PLG motion but never triggering the school upsell caps you at low six figures forever.
- Signing one big school early and letting them push you into custom features turns you into a consulting business.
- **Rule:** Don't ship the School Plan tier until you have **100 paid teachers from 30+ schools**.

### Q2: Copyright, patent, trademark

- **Copyright:** Automatic on code and content. Optional US Copyright Office registration ($65) for statutory damages.
- **Patent:** Do not attempt. Post-*Alice*, software-method patents on lesson-planning algorithms are abstract-idea unpatentable. **Publish a defensive disclosure** on your blog instead.
- **Trademark:** "Grade 5 Curriculum Planner" is unregistrable (generic + descriptive). Pick a coined or arbitrary name (Pace, Cadence, Branch, Fork, Compass, Lattice, Tutela, Praxio, etc.). Do USPTO TESS + Google + .com + Instagram/X handle check. File USPTO Class 9 (downloadable software) + Class 42 (SaaS) — ~$500–$700 in filing fees + $1,000–$2,500 if using an attorney (recommended). Madrid Protocol for international filing once you hit >$50K ARR.

### Q3: Oregon business formation + Qatar tax complexity

Oregon LLC is reasonable for a US-citizen-in-Qatar founder:
- File **Articles of Organization** with Oregon Secretary of State (~$100; $100 annual report).
- **Registered agent service** ($100–$300/yr) — required since you don't live in Oregon.
- **EIN** from IRS (free, online).
- **US business bank account** (Mercury or Relay — both work with Oregon LLCs and remote founders).
- **Operating agreement** drafted even though Oregon doesn't require one for single-member LLCs.

**Critical tax considerations (US citizen abroad):**
- **Worldwide income** is taxable to the US regardless of residence.
- LLC is pass-through by default (Schedule C unless S-corp elected).
- **FBAR / FinCEN 114** for any non-US bank account >$10K at any point in the year.
- **Form 8938 (FATCA)** for foreign financial assets above thresholds.
- **Form 2555 FEIE** for teaching salary — *but US LLC profits are generally NOT FEIE-eligible*; treated as US-source self-employment income subject to SE tax ~15.3% unless the LLC elects S-corp.
- **Qatar has no personal income tax**, so no foreign-tax-credit offset — LLC profits are fully US-taxable.
- **Strongly consider a CPA with expat experience** ($800–$1,500/yr): Greenback Expat Tax, Bright!Tax, MyExpatTaxes.

**Qatar-specific risks Frame B and Frame A both underweighted:**
- **Employment IP-assignment clause:** Many international-school Qatar contracts include broad clauses purporting to assign anything you build during employment to the school. **Read your contract before you write a single line of production code.** If such a clause exists: build outside work hours, never on school devices, never on school networks. Consider getting a written acknowledgement from the school's HR/leadership that this side project is yours.
- **Visa/sponsorship implications:** Qatari work visas are employer-sponsored. Running a registered business on the side is generally not contemplated by a standard teaching visa. A foreign-domiciled US LLC drawing revenue to a US bank account is typically considered low risk (you are not operating a business *in* Qatar), but if you ever advertise, accept Qatari customers in Qatari Riyals, or otherwise *operate* in Qatar, you may run into the requirement to register a Qatar entity (commercial registration, sponsor, etc.). **Keep the operation entirely US-domiciled** until you have legal advice or move out of Qatar.

## XV. Updated Kill Criteria

7. **Burnout signal**: If you're at <8 hrs/wk sustained for 3+ months, the project is killing you. Sabbatical for 90 days before deciding.

## XVIII. Brand & Name Lockdown — Immediate Next Steps

**Name decided: MyCurricula. Primary domain secured: mycurricula.app.**

The name is substantially more registrable than the prior working title, but still sits in the descriptive zone — "curricula" is the plural of the literal category noun. Comparable marks (MyFitnessPal, MyChart) reached the USPTO Principal Register but typically only after years of substantially exclusive use established acquired distinctiveness under Lanham Act §2(f). Expect a similar path: initial filing may be offered the Supplemental Register first, then upgradeable to the Principal Register after ~5 years of substantially exclusive use. The Supplemental Register is still useful — it gives you ®, blocks confusingly similar marks, and creates a placeholder for later Principal Register promotion.

### Trademark filing — timing matters more than urgency

**File the USPTO trademark application ~30–60 days before public paid launch** (estimated mid-2027), not now. Reasons:
- The mark must be either in use in commerce (real product + real customers + specimen of use) or filed as Intent-to-Use (ITU).
- ITU filings cost an extra $100/class plus require a Statement of Use within 6 months (extensions available for additional fees).
- Filing too early without a committed launch burns the 6-month ITU window and racks up extension fees.
- Filing too late means somebody else could file first in your category and force you to negotiate or rename.

**The 60-day-pre-launch window is the sweet spot.** By then product, screenshots, and marketing assets exist as specimens of use, and you'll qualify for a use-based filing.

### Trademark filing logistics (target: mid-2027)

- **USPTO TEAS Plus filing**: ~$250 per class. File in **both** Class 9 (downloadable software) and Class 42 (SaaS for educators) = **~$500 in filing fees**.
- **Attorney-assisted filing** (strongly recommended for descriptive-zone marks): $1,000–$2,500 additional. A flat-fee trademark attorney (Cognition IP, LegalForce, or a solo IP practitioner) will draft the description of goods/services and respond to Office Actions — both highly likely for marks in the descriptive zone.
- **Madrid Protocol international filing**: defer until $50K+ ARR. Filing starts ~$650 + per-country fees.
- **Time to ®**: 12–18 months from filing to registration in a clean case; 18–30 months if any Office Action is issued.

### Backup-name optionality

Have **2 backup-domain registrations** as cheap insurance in case USPTO blocks MyCurricula on the Principal Register and you decide a coined/arbitrary mark is worth the rebrand cost later. Verify availability before committing — common candidates include `pacely.app`, `forka.app`, `coursely.app`, `praxio.app`, `curriculate.app`. Spending $30–$60 now on two defensive registrations is cheap optionality.
