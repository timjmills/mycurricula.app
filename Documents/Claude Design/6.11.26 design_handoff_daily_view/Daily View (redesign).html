Math

Fractions as Division

Objective

Students will interpret a fraction as division of the numerator by the denominator and model a sharing problem two ways — with a bar diagram and an equation.

Standards

5.NF.B.3Interpret a fraction as division of the numerator by the denominator (a/b = a ÷ b); solve word problems involving division of whole numbers leading to fraction answers.
MP.4Model with mathematics — represent a sharing situation with both a diagram and an equation.

Lesson Notes

Pull aside the small group still on the array model for a quick reteach. If they struggle, fall back to the strip diagrams from Lesson 22. Save two contrasting student samples for the share.

Differentiation

Support

Pre-cut fraction strips and sentence frames: “___ wholes shared by ___ is ___.”

On Level

Model with a bar diagram, then write the matching division equation independently.

Extension

Problems where the answer is greater than one whole; rename as a mixed number.

Teacher Instructions

Project the bake-sale photo on slide 3. Pose the anchor question: “If 3 brownies are shared equally among 4 friends, how much does each friend get?” Let students estimate before any calculation.

Student Prompt

Turn & talk — is each share more or less than one whole brownie? How do you know?

Teacher Instructions

Model that sharing 3 wholes among 4 people is written 3 ÷ 4. Cut each brownie into fourths on the bar diagram and deal one piece to each friend — every friend collects 3 pieces, or three-fourths.

Example 1

Three brownies shared by four friends.

3 ÷ 4 = 34

Example 2

Two granola bars shared by three students.

2 ÷ 3 = 23

Check for Understanding

  • What does the numerator represent in a sharing problem?
  • How does the bar model show “3 divided by 4”?

Teacher Instructions

Pairs solve two sharing problems on whiteboards. Require both a bar diagram and a matching division equation for each.

Student Task

Share 5 sandwiches among 4 people, then 7 apples among 2 people. Decide whether each answer is more or less than one whole.

Teacher Look-fors

  • Equal-sized partitions in the bar diagram.
  • Numerator and denominator matched to the story.
  • Recognizing answers greater than one whole.
