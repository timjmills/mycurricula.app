00 · Sensibility

Aesthetic sensibilities

The felt sense of "this is Curricula." When a decision is ambiguous, lean here. The premium feeling comes from restraint — daylight, glass, air, and color that whispers before it speaks.

Daylight, not screens
Morning light through a window — warm whites, soft shadows, glass that catches light. Never flat cold grey.
Restraint = quality
The premium feeling comes from what we leave out. One focal element per surface; empty space is a feature.
Depth by light, not lines
Separate with translucency, blur, and soft shadow — not hard dividers. Layered glass, not stacked boxes.
Color whispers, then speaks
Resting state is near-neutral. Color enters only to carry meaning, and then it's confident — one color leads at a time.
Rule #1 — No sharp corners, ever
Every panel, card, chip, tab, button, image, preview tile and input is rounded — no exceptions. When in doubt, round it. Feathered shadows, gentle gradients, eased motion follow from the same instinct: soft everything.
Motion is breath
Alive but at rest — a slow drift, a gentle settle. If an animation calls attention to itself, it's too much.
Type is calm
Generous line-height, open tracking, lighter weights over bold. Words have room; headings are quiet display.
Tactile & physical
Glass, light, momentum — surfaces respond, follow the finger, and settle. Digital with the weight of something real.
Honest & humane
No dark patterns, no fake urgency, no decorative data. Everything on screen is true, useful, and respects attention.
Gut-checks before shipping
Does it feel light? Could I remove something and lose nothing? Is there one clear focus? Does color mean something here? Would it feel at home next to the home screen? Any "no" → keep refining.

03 · Materials

Glass, radius & elevation

Glass is the signature material — frosted, translucent panels with an inner top highlight that reads as a lit edge. Corners are always rounded (no sharp corners, ever). Shadows are wide, faint, and cool. Calm Glass ships in two teacher-selectable registers — Dark frosted (white text, cinematic) and White frosted (ink text, airy) — set with data-glass. Over a photo both use the plain frosted material; over a wash both use the Liquid v5 finish: a flat liquid body with a sharp bright top edge, deep float shadow and a glowing subject rail.

04 · Themes

Seven themes

A theme washes over the whole app — background palette, a gentle tint over everything, and the accent/glow color. One tone governs the entire app so every page matches: Night is dark, everything else light. All washes drift slowly by default.

04 · Themes

Surface theming — every surface follows the options

Every surface, menu, panel, page and feature must display the appearance options appropriately (unless a surface is deliberately pinned to a fixed look — e.g. the Teach projection stage or the print/export viewer). The three axes have different natural reach, so classify each surface into a tier. Modals/popovers sit above the app-wide wash, so they get their own faint accent wash centrally (see home/themes.css → "SURFACE THEMING CONTRACT").

Tier 1 · Major surfaces

Full-screen wall · Planner hub · Tools dock · lesson/resource left panel · Config & Settings · Catch-up · Unit/Lesson planner.
Theme (tone + accent + wash): always. Background (Photo/Wash): when full-bleed. Frame (Glass/Bright/Color): on data-layout surfaces.

Tier 2 · Lesser menus

Right-click menus · popovers · dropdowns · small dialogs · quote popup · row menus.
Theme: always (accent + a whisper of wash). Background & Frame: never — a small menu floats above the background and has no layout character; forcing them on is noise and a legibility risk.

Build rule: branch on data-tone (never a specific theme), build from the glass recipe + var(--accent)/subject tokens (never hard-code a color), and verify over Wash · Photo-Dim · Photo-Bright · Night before shipping. A new overlay must reuse a registered surface class or add its root + scrim to the central rule. Full details in V2 Framework.md §15.

09 · Combinations

The legibility contract

Non-negotiable. Every combination must satisfy this — when a surface flips tone, its text flips with it.

White on dark
Night · Photo Dim/Normal
Dark tone → white text
Translucent-dark glass; dark-glass menus/popups; subject cells deepen so white labels hold.
Ink on light
Wash · Photo Bright · light themes
Light tone → ink text
White/translucent-white cards; the wash or photo tint sits over text without recoloring it.
Accent ≠ body text
The theme accent colors interactive + emphasis elements only (primary buttons, active tab, now-ring, focus). Never plain reading text or nav labels — those stay neutral so the wash passes over them.
Subject color always wins
For subject identity, on any background: solid/ink over light, bright variant or deepened gradient over dark.
Never cross tones
No ink text on a dark scrim; no white text on a white card. That's what the Bright override exists to prevent.

Avoid · and the forward rule

Minimal canvas over a busy Bright photo
Floating text loses its ground. Pair Minimal with Dim/Normal or Wash.
Frame C under a strong color theme on Photo-Dim
Gradients + dark photo + heavy accent = muddy. Use C on light tone.
Dark pills on a light Wash
Heavy and out of character. Prefer Segmented or Light.
Two saturated layers at once
Vivid wash + vivid panels + colored accent. Let one layer carry color; neutralize the rest.

For new menus & functions

Any surface added later must inherit the mix, not fight it. Follow these and a new feature drops into any combination looking like it was always there.

1 · Read tone, not theme
Branch on data-tone (light/dark), never a specific theme — that's what stays correct across all seven themes automatically.
2 · Use glass + tokens
Build from the glass/card recipe and var(--accent)/subject tokens. Never hard-code a color or a white/black.
3 · Respect the legibility contract
Text flips with tone; accent only on interactive/emphasis; subject color for identity.
4 · Default neutral, earn color
A new control arrives quiet; it picks up the accent only for its primary action or active state.
5 · Survive every background
Verify over Wash, Photo-Dim, Photo-Bright, and Night — the four tone + contrast extremes.
6 · Round, float, one job
Rounded corners, floating, single-purpose, progressive disclosure — always.
10 · Touch & swipe

Everything touchable & swipable

A tablet-and-phone product as much as a desktop one — a teacher holds an iPad at the board. Every interaction has a finger-native path at least as good as the mouse path. Build with Pointer Events; if you can't do it with a finger, it isn't done.

Tap = click
Every target tappable, ≥44px, no reliance on hover. Hover-only reveals (the week + button, lesson hover card) also surface on tap / long-press.
Swipe navigates
Horizontal swipe moves between console views, days, weeks, and Teach slides. Vertical swipe scrolls within a surface — never the page.
Drag by finger
Tab reorder, resource drag-to-board, the Teach resizer, slide reordering — all touch-drag via Pointer Events, never mouse-only.
Long-press = secondary
Surfaces the onboarding explanation on touch and opens context actions (skip / duplicate / delete) where desktop uses right-click.
Pinch / pull
Pinch-zoom the Teach board; pull-to-dismiss (swipe-down) the settings drawer and sheet popups.
Momentum & snap
Swipe transitions follow the finger then snap to the nearest view with easing — no dead, instant cuts on touch.
No hover-only state
Hover may enhance on desktop, but the feature must be fully operable by tap / swipe / long-press.
Generous spacing
Touch rows and chips get larger hit areas and gaps than a mouse design — err roomy on phone & tablet.
