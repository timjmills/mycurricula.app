# AI Gameplan for MyCurricula
### Competitive teardown of Common Planner's AI + a build plan for our own

*Prepared July 16, 2026. Based on live inspection of Common Planner (commonplanner.com) while logged in, plus current API pricing research.*

---

## 1. Bottom line up front

Common Planner's AI is **still live** (I nearly missed it too — it's a small "AI" chip on each lesson card, badged "NEW"). Under the hood, the evidence points strongly to **Google Gemini, Flash tier**, called server-side and streamed to the browser over their real-time WebSocket. It is **agentic** — it doesn't just chat, it writes generated content directly into structured lesson fields.

Critically, they generate at **two levels of granularity**: (1) **per lesson part** — fill or improve a single card (Objective, Engage, etc.), and (2) **per whole lesson** — generate an entire lesson at once as a model/template. **They do not have unit-level generation.** That gap is our opening.

For us, the play is not to copy it but to **ship a sharper, better-integrated version on a newer model, with a third tier they lack — whole-unit generation.** The economics make even frontier-lite models affordable at our scale, and their model is visibly a generation behind.

## 2. What AI are they using? (Evidence + confidence)

**Conclusion: Google Gemini, Flash tier (likely 1.5/2.0 Flash-class), almost certainly via Google Vertex AI. Confidence: high, but not proven.**

How we know:

| Probe | Result | What it tells us |
|---|---|---|
| "What model/company are you?" | *"I am Gemini, a large language model built by Google."* | Direct, specific, unprompted-toward-Google identity |
| "Knowledge cutoff + latest event?" | *"late 2023 to early 2024… April 8, 2024 solar eclipse"* | Matches Gemini 1.5/2.0 Flash-era cutoff — an **older, cheaper** model, not the newest |
| "Which Gemini version?" | *"Gemini"* (refused specifics) | Version masked by their system prompt (normal) |
| Network trace during generation | **Zero** calls to any AI vendor; only a Segment analytics ping | AI runs 100% server-side over their WebSocket — provider is hidden from the client by design |

**Important caveat:** models can hallucinate their own identity, and a vendor's system prompt can *assign* a persona. But the direct Google claim, the matching knowledge cutoff, their existing deep Google integration (Google Classroom + Drive already wired in), and the cost logic below all point the same way. The only *proof* would be their subprocessor list (request-only via privacy@commonplanner.com).

**Why Vertex AI specifically:** they handle FERPA/COPPA student data. Vertex AI offers data-residency, a DPA, and a no-training-on-your-data guarantee that a school-data company needs. The consumer Gemini API is a weaker fit for that compliance posture.

## 3. Cost & scale analysis — why they chose Flash

Common Planner serves **300,000+ teachers on a freemium model.** On a free tier, per-user AI cost has to round to pennies per year or it destroys margin. That single constraint explains the model choice.

**Rough cost per AI action** (assume ~3,000 input tokens of lesson/planbook context + ~300 output tokens):

| Model | Input / Output ($/1M) | Cost per action | Relative |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite | $0.10 / $0.40 | **~$0.0004** | 1× (baseline) |
| GPT-4o-mini | $0.15 / $0.60 | ~$0.0006 | 1.4× |
| GPT-5 mini | $0.25 / $2.00 | ~$0.0014 | 3.2× |
| Frontier-lite (e.g. Gemini 3.5 Flash $1.50/$9) | — | ~$0.0072 | **17×** |

**At scale** (300k teachers × ~20 AI actions/month = 6M calls/month):

- Flash-Lite tier: **~$2,500/month** — negligible.
- GPT-5 mini: ~$8,000/month.
- Frontier-lite: ~$43,000/month.

The takeaway: even a 17× step up to a frontier-lite model is *only* ~$43k/month at their entire user base — real, but not scary. **The cost objection to using a better model than they do is weak.** Their older-Flash choice looks like it was locked in a while ago and left alone, not a decision they'd re-make today.

**Cost by generation granularity** (Flash-Lite tier, illustrative):

| Scope | Typical in / out tokens | Cost per generation |
|---|---|---|
| Lesson part (one card) | ~3k / ~300 | ~$0.0004 |
| Whole lesson | ~3k / ~1,200 | ~$0.0008 |
| **Whole unit (8–12 lessons)** | ~5k / ~12,000 | **~$0.005** |

Even unit-level generation is **half a cent** on Flash-Lite. A teacher generating a few full units a year costs pennies. **Cost is not the constraint at unit level — quality, latency, and control are.** A unit is too big to one-shot blindly, so the design (below) uses a staged outline-then-expand pipeline with a teacher approval gate, not a single mega-prompt.

*Pricing is approximate and changes often — verify against Google Cloud and OpenAI pricing pages before finalizing budgets.*

## 4. Feature teardown — what they actually built

The UX and architecture, from direct inspection:

- **Entry point:** a small "AI" button on every lesson card (not a separate page). Low friction, in-context.
- **Quick actions (chips):** *Differentiate · Improve · Give me Ideas · Add questions · Add homework.* One tap, no prompt-writing required.
- **Free-text prompt box** for anything else.
- **Two generation scopes:**
  - **Per lesson part** — fill/improve a single card. When asked to "write a 120-word paragraph," it replied *"I have… placed it in the Explain card"* — it targets **specific fields of the 5E structure** (Objective, Engage, Explore, Explain, Extend, Evaluate) via function/tool calling.
  - **Per whole lesson** — generate an entire lesson at once as a model/template (confirmed by Tim), i.e. all cards populated in one pass.
- **No unit-level generation.** Their ceiling is a single lesson. Sequencing a multi-lesson unit is still manual.
- **Context-aware:** their marketing claims it uses "context from your recent lessons and unit plans." Gemini's large context window makes stuffing that context cheap.
- **Transport:** streamed over their existing collaboration WebSocket (`/socket/v4`), same channel as real-time co-editing.

**What's weak / beatable:**

- **No unit-level generation** — the single biggest gap, and exactly where we want to lead.
- The model is a generation behind (early-2024 cutoff) — outputs were competent but generic.
- Quick actions are shallow (generic verbs), not pedagogy-aware.
- No visible standards-alignment in the generation loop (they *track* standards elsewhere but the AI didn't cite them).
- Provider lock is invisible to users — no "powered by" trust signal either way.

## 5. Recommended stack for MyCurricula

**Model:** Start on **Gemini 2.5 Flash / Flash-Lite via Vertex AI** — matches their cost profile, gives us the same Google-for-Education compliance story and Classroom/Drive synergy, and is already *newer* than what they run. Architect the model call behind a thin abstraction so we can **A/B a sharper model (GPT-5 mini / Claude Haiku-class) on paid tiers** without re-plumbing.

**Why an abstraction layer matters:** their entire provider is swappable server-side and invisible to users — we should build the same optionality from day one. It lets us route cheap models to free users and premium models to paid/school tiers, and renegotiate vendors freely.

**Transport:** stream tokens (SSE or WebSocket). Users expect live typing; batch responses feel broken by comparison.

**Compliance:** Vertex AI DPA + no-training flag, data residency, and a written subprocessor entry. This is table stakes for selling to schools/districts and is a place we can be *more transparent* than they are.

## 6. Feature design spec — a three-tier generation model

Organize the whole feature around **three scopes of generation**, sharing one model layer, one context engine, and one preview/accept UX. Common Planner has the first two; **unit-level (Tier 3) is our headline capability.**

### Tier 1 — Lesson part (card level) *— parity*
- In-context "AI" affordance on each planning card/field; low-friction, like theirs.
- **3–5 pedagogy-aware quick actions** (beat their generic verbs): *Differentiate by level · Simplify for ELL · Add a check for understanding · Align to standard · Generate examples.*
- Free-text prompt fallback.
- Writes into the correct structured field with **preview + accept/undo** (never silent insert — §7).

### Tier 2 — Whole lesson *— parity+*
- One action generates a **complete lesson**: all fields populated coherently in a single pass (objective → activities → assessment aligned to each other).
- Seeded from grade, subject, selected standard(s), and a one-line teacher intent.
- Show the full lesson as a **reviewable draft**; accept all / edit / regenerate per card.
- Edge over them: **standards drive the generation**, and the objective/assessment are checked for alignment before display.

### Tier 3 — Whole unit *— our differentiator (they don't have this)*
This is a **staged, agentic pipeline**, not a single mega-prompt — a unit is too big to one-shot reliably:

1. **Inputs:** unit topic/title, grade, subject, the **standards to cover**, number of lessons or a date range, and pacing (periods/week).
2. **Step 1 — Outline (approval gate):** AI proposes a **lesson-by-lesson sequence** using backward design (end goal + summative assessment first, then sequenced lessons), with a **standards-coverage map** showing every required standard is distributed and nothing is orphaned. Teacher edits/reorders/approves *before* any lessons are written.
3. **Step 2 — Expand:** on approval, generate each lesson (Tier 2 quality) across the sequence, keeping pacing, prerequisites, and a coherent arc — later lessons build on earlier ones.
4. **Step 3 — Review & place:** drop the unit onto the planner/calendar as drafts; accept all or refine lesson-by-lesson.

**Why staged matters:** it controls quality and latency, gives the teacher agency (they own the arc), and makes cost predictable. It also produces the standards-coverage artifact that admins/districts love — a built-in sales asset.

### Cross-cutting: context engine
Pass current lesson, unit goals, grade, subject, **selected standards**, and (summarized) recent planbook history into every tier. Standards-native generation across all three tiers is our clearest wedge over their standards-blind AI.

### V2 add-ons
- **Model tiering:** premium model for paid/school seats — the quality gap becomes a sales lever.
- **"Powered responsibly" trust panel:** name our subprocessor, no-training guarantee, FERPA posture. Turn the thing they hide into a differentiator for district buyers.
- **Feedback loop:** thumbs up/down per generation to tune prompts and prune low-value quick actions.

### Guardrails & cost controls
- Cap/​summarize context tokens rather than dumping full planbook history.
- Rate-limit free tier; gate Tier 3 (unit) behind paid/school seats or a monthly quota — it's the highest-value, and a natural upsell.
- Cache common generations (standard-aligned objectives, exit tickets).
- Log accept/reject rates per tier and per quick-action.

## 7. Who gets AI — free for all, or paid only?

This is a **monetization and growth decision, not a cost decision.** Section 3 showed AI is pennies even at unit scale, so we are *not* forced to paywall it to protect margin. The real question is: does AI do more for us as an **acquisition/activation engine** (give it away) or as a **premium upsell** (gate it)?

**Option A — Free for all users.** Maximizes adoption, word-of-mouth, and activation; matches a "free forever" base tier; makes AI a habit before we ask for money. But it gives away our differentiator, offers no upgrade hook, and — if the whole feature is free — leaves money on the table since teachers increasingly *expect* to pay for AI.

**Option B — Paid users only.** Clean monetization and protects premium value. But it kneecaps top-of-funnel in a market where free AI planners exist, makes AI invisible to the users most likely to evangelize it, and risks looking behind if competitors surface AI on their free tier.

**Option C — Freemium hybrid *(recommended)*.** Let *everyone* taste AI, but **meter it, and reserve the highest-value tier for paid.** This maps cleanly onto our three-tier design:

| Capability | Free tier | Paid / School seats |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 — card-level AI (quick actions) | ✅ with a **monthly quota** (e.g. 20–30 generations) | ✅ unlimited |
| Tier 2 — whole-lesson generation | ✅ **limited** (e.g. 3–5 lessons/month) | ✅ unlimited |
| **Tier 3 — whole-unit generation** | 🚫 (or 1 trial unit) | ✅ **the flagship paid capability** |
| Model quality | standard (Flash-Lite) | **premium model** on paid seats |
| Standards-coverage map / district features | — | ✅ |

**Why C wins for us:**

- **AI becomes the conversion engine, not a cost center.** Free users get hooked on card- and lesson-level help; the upgrade ask is *volume* + *unit-level generation* + *sharper model* — all things a heavy user genuinely wants.
- **We monetize where the value and effort are highest** (Tier 3 units, premium quality, district tooling), and give away the cheap, habit-forming parts.
- **It neutralizes the competitor either way.** If Common Planner gates AI, our free-tier AI wins the funnel. If they give it away free, we match on the free tier *and* still out-monetize them with unit-level generation they don't have.
- **Cost stays trivial** because free-tier quotas + rate limits cap the tail, and Tier 3 (the only pricier call) is paid-gated.

**Open decisions for the team** (worth settling before build): exact free-tier quota numbers; whether unit generation gets a one-time free trial to drive conversion; and whether school/district seats unlock everything unlimited (recommended — that's where the real revenue is, and buyers expect an all-in AI story).

**Metric to watch:** free→paid conversion *attributable to hitting an AI limit*. If teachers upgrade after bumping the quota or wanting a full unit, the hybrid is working.

### Benchmark — what Common Planner actually does (confirmed from their pricing page)

They already run the hybrid, and it validates our approach:

| | Basic (Free) | Pro ($5.59/mo, $71.88/yr) | Schools |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI assistant | ✅ **limited usage** | ✅ **2× the AI usage** | ✅ + admin |
| Unit planning | 🚫 | ✅ **paid-gated** | ✅ |
| Auto standards tracking | 🚫 | ✅ | ✅ |
| Google Classroom posts | 50 | unlimited | unlimited |
| Collaboration / class website / multi-level print | 🚫 | ✅ | ✅ (school-wide) |

Two takeaways: **AI is their hook, not their paywall** — it's *metered* (2× on Pro), because at pennies-per-use a free allowance is just a cheap, viral demo. And **unit planning is already paid-only even in its manual form** — which strongly supports gating our Tier 3 unit *AI generation* behind paid.

**Price flag:** their flagship individual plan is **$5.59/mo**, *below* our proposed $7.99 AI tier. A $7.99 price must be justified by capabilities they lack — unit-level AI generation and a premium model — or we should consider pricing at/near theirs. Decide this deliberately.

### Suggested pricing ladder — priced to undercut them and still profit

Goal: **stay under Common Planner's ~$5.59 AI plan while keeping strong margin.** Full unit economics are in the companion model (`mycurricula-pricing-model.xlsx`); summary below.

| Plan | Price (monthly) | Gross margin* | What's included |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Free** | $0 | — | Core planning + metered AI taste (Tier 1 quota, a few Tier 2 lessons) |
| **Basic — no AI** | **$2.99** | ~72% | Full premium planning (unlimited units, sharing, collaboration, print/export) — **no AI** |
| **Plus — AI included** | **$4.99** | **~78%** | Everything in Basic + **unlimited Tier 1 & 2 AI, Tier 3 unit generation, premium model**. Undercuts their $5.59 *and* does more. |
| **School / District** | per-seat (sales) | highest | Everything unlimited + standards-coverage map & admin tooling |

*\*Gross margin = per-user variable margin (AI + Stripe + infra + support). Fixed costs covered by volume.*

**Why this is profitable under their price:**

- **AI costs only ~$0.20 / paid user / month** (§3) — even for a heavy user on the premium model. It is *not* the cost driver.
- **The real costs at low price points are Stripe fees and infra**, ~$0.85/month combined on a $4.99 monthly plan. Even so, Plus at **$4.99 nets ~$3.90/user/month (~78% margin)** — vs their $5.59 plan we undercut by $0.60 and still keep a fat margin.
- **Push annual billing.** Stripe's $0.30 fixed fee is brutal on cheap monthly plans (10% of a $2.99 charge). Annual amortizes it to ~$0.01/month, *raising* margin to ~81% even at a discounted effective price. Offer annual at ~2 months free.
- **Even an aggressive $3.99 AI tier holds ~73% margin** — so there's room to drop price further in a price war and still profit.

**The lever to decide:** how hard to undercut. **$4.99 Plus** is the sweet spot — clearly cheaper than $5.59, obviously more capable (unit AI they lack), and ~78% margin. Go to **$3.99** only if winning the price comparison outright matters more than per-user margin; you still profit, just ~$1/user/month less.

**A non-AI Basic at $2.99** captures price-sensitive and AI-skeptical teachers instead of losing them, and sits well below their $5.59 — a second way we look cheaper.

## 8. How we beat them — the short list

1. **Unit-level generation they don't have.** Their ceiling is one lesson; we generate a full, sequenced, standards-mapped unit via backward design. This is the flagship differentiator.
2. **Newer model, visibly sharper output.** They're on an early-2024-cutoff Flash. Costs let us go one tier better.
3. **Standards-native generation** across all three tiers. Bake standards alignment into the AI loop, not a separate tracker. This is our curriculum DNA.
4. **Preview-and-accept, not silent insert.** Their AI wrote into a card with no confirmation step — a trust/UX gap we can own.
5. **Transparent AI trust story** for district buyers (subprocessor, no-training, FERPA) — the opposite of their hidden provider.
6. **Deeper pedagogy in quick actions** (ELL, SPED, tiering) rather than generic "Improve/Give me Ideas."

---

## 9. Phased rollout

- **Phase 0 (now):** stand up Vertex AI access + DPA; build the shared model-abstraction layer, streaming transport, and context engine.
- **Phase 1 (Tier 1 — parity):** card-level AI with 5 pedagogy-aware quick actions + preview/accept, standards context injected.
- **Phase 2 (Tier 2 — parity+):** whole-lesson generation as a reviewable draft, standards-aligned.
- **Phase 3 (Tier 3 — differentiator):** whole-unit generation via the staged outline→approve→expand pipeline with a standards-coverage map; gate behind paid/school seats.
- **Phase 4:** model tiering, trust/compliance panel, feedback loop, and district-facing positioning. North-star metrics: generation **accept-rate** and **cost-per-active-teacher**.
